A Nairobi hair salon books twelve appointments per week through WhatsApp. Each booking begins with a conversation. The customer names a service, proposes a time, and receives a confirmation. Then the thread is joined by eleven others. By Thursday, the Tuesday conversation is invisible without scrolling.

Three kinds of revenue disappear in this scenario. The customer who did not receive a reminder and did not show. The customer who asked about a new service and received no follow-up. The customer who rescheduled once and was never contacted again.

Each loss is small. Together, across a year, they are the difference between a growing business and a flat one.

---

Appointment-based businesses have a property that most micro-businesses do not: the cost of a missed follow-up is specific and time-bound.

A fashion seller who fails to follow up on a WhatsApp inquiry loses revenue - but the timing is diffuse. The customer might still buy next week, next month, or never. The loss is real but difficult to isolate.

A hair salon that fails to send a reminder loses a confirmed booking. The appointment time passes. The slot is empty. The revenue was as certain as micro-business revenue gets, and it is gone at a specific moment.

This specificity makes appointment businesses the clearest initial customer for follow-up software in Africa.

<Pullquote>
The wedge is not the easiest customer. It is the customer where the product value is most visible on day one.
</Pullquote>

Salons, tutors, coaches, physiotherapists, event caterers, and photographers share this structure. Every customer inquiry has an implied timeline. Every booking has a confirmation that is never automated. Every no-show is a revenue event that is counted as a bad day rather than a system failure.

The initial ICP is not all African micro-businesses. It is the subset where missing a follow-up has a known cost and a known timing. That subset is large enough to build on and concentrated enough to reach.

---

<Factblock title="What appointment businesses already have">
A customer who has booked is more committed than a lead. They have named a time. They have agreed to a price. The confirmation is the last step before revenue. A reminder system that fires 24 hours before the appointment does not need to convince anyone of anything. It keeps an already-closed deal from falling through.
</Factblock>

The product specification follows from the constraint. Appointment businesses need three things: a booking list, a pre-appointment reminder, and a post-appointment follow-up. The third element is where expansion revenue lives. A client who returns is worth more than one who books once.

The interaction is always WhatsApp. The owner books on WhatsApp, confirms on WhatsApp, and follows up on WhatsApp. The product that wins sits beside this workflow. It does not ask the owner to adopt a new channel or log interactions in a separate system.

One tap to confirm a booking. One tap to send a pre-appointment reminder. One tap to follow up the day after. The product is a structured timing layer on top of conversations that are already happening.

The appointment business wedge is not about building the entire African micro-business SaaS market from day one. It is about building the product appointment business owners will use every day. The business logic learned there extends to every other micro-business category.

The path from salon reminder tool to general micro-business follow-up system is shorter than starting from enterprise CRM. The wedge determines the learning curve.

Appointment businesses already know they need this. The product just needs to exist in a form they can find.
