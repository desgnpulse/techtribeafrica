A Lagos textile importer who needs $300,000 in USDT to clear a container from Guangzhou does not open an app. They call a broker they have used for two years and negotiate a rate. They send naira to a local account and receive USDT to their business wallet within two hours. No KYC form. No API. No onboarding flow. A relationship, a phone, and a rate.

This is how the majority of African B2B stablecoin on-ramp volume moves. Sub-Saharan Africa processed over $205 billion in on-chain stablecoin transactions between July 2024 and June 2025, a 52% year-on-year increase. The infrastructure underlying most of that volume is informal OTC desks. They operate on trust and reputation, not licensed platforms or regulated on-ramp products.

---

The formal on-ramp landscape is thin. Yellow Card operates across 20 African markets and dominates institutional API volume. Paychant, HoneyCoin, and SCRYPT are building licensed settlement infrastructure. SCRYPT expanded across four East African markets in July 2026. Rach Finance opened an OTC desk for stablecoin payments in January 2026. None of these has captured the $50,000-to-$500,000 per-transaction range that characterizes importer-level demand.

The structural reason is trust. An importer moving $300,000 through a platform they signed up for last week is not making a rational decision. That transaction requires three forms of confidence. The counterparty will deliver USDT after receiving naira. The rate will not shift mid-execution. A dispute has a resolution path. No onboarding flow creates that confidence. A two-year relationship with a broker does.

Accrue, a Lagos-based startup, reached this diagnosis in late 2025 and built around it. Rather than an app, it built a human agent network. Physical agents hold float, manage local currency conversion, and process cash-to-stablecoin transactions for business clients. Its B2B product raised $1.58 million in seed in mid-2026 and has processed $70-100 million in total volume.

The regulatory environment is also shifting. In 2025, the Nigerian SEC issued the first Digital Asset Service Provider licences. These created a formal legal structure for stablecoin on-ramp operators. Kenya, Ghana, and Uganda are developing equivalent frameworks. The regulatory scaffold for a licensed, trust-grade on-ramp business now exists in the markets where demand is highest.

---

The product gap is specific. It is not retail consumer on-ramp, which several platforms serve adequately. It is not institutional API access, which Yellow Card and Flutterwave cover. It is SME-scale on-ramp with compliance documentation, credit integration, and relationship management built in.

An importer moving $2 million in USDT per month through an OTC desk has no audit trail for their accountant. No credit facility bridges the timing gap between naira payment and USDT receipt. No integration connects the transaction to their trade documentation workflow. Those three gaps represent the upgrade path from informal broker call to structured product.

The business that builds this is not a crypto exchange. It is closer to a trade finance desk with stablecoin settlement as the execution layer. Revenue comes from FX conversion margin, a credit fee on the settlement bridge, and a SaaS compliance layer fee.

The African importer is not waiting for stablecoins to become easier to use. They are already using stablecoins, at scale, through brokers. The product that wins this market formalizes the broker relationship without removing the trust that makes it work.
