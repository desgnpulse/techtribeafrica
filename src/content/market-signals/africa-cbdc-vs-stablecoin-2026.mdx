The Nigerian government spent four years building the eNaira. Eleven percent of Nigerians ignored it and bought USDT instead.

That gap is not a UX problem. It is a trust problem embedded in the product architecture.

---

Nigeria launched the eNaira in October 2021. It is the continent's only fully deployed retail CBDC. As of late 2024, 0.5 percent of Nigeria's population had used it. Ninety-eight and a half percent of all registered eNaira wallets had never been used, per IMF data. The CBN's own officials confirmed the trajectory in November 2025: the eNaira is "not a rosy story."

Stablecoins tell a different story from the same period. Sub-Saharan Africa now leads the world in stablecoin adoption. Nine point three percent of the region's residents use them, according to the Milken Institute. In Nigeria specifically, 11.9 percent of the population holds or transacts in stablecoins. That is approximately 25.9 million people.

Ghana's eCedi has not reached full retail launch as of 2026. Eleven African countries are exploring or piloting CBDCs. Nigeria is the only one that went all the way to market. The outcome was a product in technical operation and practical disuse.

---

The CBDC failure is not a technology failure. The eNaira runs on functional infrastructure. The failure is a design failure rooted in a trust deficit.

Nigeria's naira lost approximately 70 percent of its value between 2021 and 2024. Nigerian adults who lived through that devaluation are asked to hold a digital version of the same naira. Issued by the same central bank. The digital form does not dissolve that institutional history. It carries it.

Surveillance is the second design problem. CBDCs are centralised. Every transaction is visible to the issuing authority. In markets with documented governance vulnerabilities, this is not an abstract concern. It is the reason citizens prefer a payment system that does not report to the central bank.

Stablecoins solve both problems at the architecture level. USDT and USDC are dollar-denominated. They hold their value against the currency Nigerians actually trust. They are issued by private entities without state transaction visibility. They work on OPay and similar platforms that have already built the distribution layer.

A stablecoin does not require citizens to change their view of their government. It requires them to open an app they are already using.

---

The CBN's response to the eNaira's failure is instructive. In 2025, the CBN shifted focus toward a wholesale CBDC model. It also cleared the path for cNGN: a private-sector naira-pegged stablecoin with SEC oversight. cNGN is positioned as interoperable with the eNaira. The practical effect is clear: the central bank is now backing the model the market proved out. Not the one it designed.

The pattern across Africa is that CBDC projects are stalling while private stablecoin infrastructure is scaling. Flutterwave partnered with Polygon Labs in October 2025 to enable USDC and USDT transfers across its network. Visa expanded stablecoin settlement across the CEMEA region in June 2025. The institutional infrastructure is moving toward stablecoins. African central banks are not leading that movement. They are following it.

---

The signal for builders is in the structure of the trust problem. CBDCs failed because they asked citizens to trust institutions they have reason to distrust. The products that succeeded separated the settlement layer from the state.

The opportunity is not to compete with CBDCs. It is to build the private-sector layer that CBDCs were supposed to provide. Compliance-ready stablecoin corridors. On-ramp tooling that works on feature phones. Treasury management for businesses that need to hold USD value without dollar bank accounts.

African central banks will not stop exploring CBDCs. But the adoption data has established which side of this contest the market has chosen. The builders who align with that choice are building on the ground the market already settled.
