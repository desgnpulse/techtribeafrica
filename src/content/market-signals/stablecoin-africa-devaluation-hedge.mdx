A Lagos trader converted her January salary into USDT. The Central Bank of Nigeria had just abandoned its fixed exchange rate in June 2023.

The naira fell from NGN 464 to the dollar to NGN 708 in a single week. By February 2024, it had passed NGN 1,400. She was not speculating on crypto. She was protecting savings from a currency she could see falling in real time.

---

Between July 2023 and June 2024, Nigeria processed $22 billion in stablecoin transactions. That volume was not driven by DeFi yield farming or token speculation. Chainalysis data shows stablecoins accounted for 43 percent of all crypto transaction volume across Sub-Saharan Africa in that period. In Nigeria, 38.3 percent of all crypto sent by Nigerians was in stablecoins. Dollar-pegged tokens, held as savings, not traded for yield.

Nigeria processed $59 billion in total crypto transactions across that period - second globally only to India. Sub-Saharan Africa as a region reached $205 billion in on-chain value, a 52 percent year-on-year increase. The growth was not evenly distributed. It concentrated where currency instability was highest.

---

The global stablecoin narrative is a DeFi narrative. Yield protocols. Liquidity pools. Collateralised positions. The African stablecoin narrative is different. It is a savings narrative.

Ghana's cedi and the Nigerian naira both depreciated significantly between 2022 and 2024. Kenyan inflation averaged above 7 percent through 2023. In each market, stablecoin adoption moves with local currency performance, not with global crypto sentiment. When local currency falls and the dollar holds, USDT becomes a savings vehicle. Opening a dollar account in Nigeria requires documentation most working adults do not have. A stablecoin wallet does not.

The CBN moved from an outright crypto ban to a regulated framework between 2023 and 2025. The ISA 2025 recognised digital assets as securities under SEC oversight. Nigeria launched cNGN, its first domestically issued stablecoin, in early 2025 within a CBN sandbox. The regulatory direction is toward structured integration, not prohibition.

---

The product that wins this market is not an exchange. It is a savings account that holds its value in a currency that holds its value.

The Africa stablecoin user is a professional with a salary denominated in a depreciating currency. She has found a hedge that requires no bank relationship and no foreign exchange bureau queue. The demand is structural, not speculative. The product opportunity is in meeting that demand with tools designed for the actual use case. Not the one described in a Silicon Valley whitepaper.
