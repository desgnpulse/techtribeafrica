A Lagos textile exporter has a client in Nairobi. She ships the goods. The payment takes five business days to clear and costs seven percent of the transfer value.

This is not a crypto story. It is a trade infrastructure story.

---

Intra-African trade accounts for 16 percent of the continent's total trade. The European Union moves 70 percent of its trade internally. Asia moves 60 percent. Africa's number reflects a settlement layer designed for export to Europe and North America. Not for the commerce between Lagos and Nairobi, Accra and Addis Ababa, Dakar and Dar es Salaam.

The cost is not abstract. Africa pays $5 billion annually to correspondent banking intermediaries. The trade finance gap - the unmet demand for trade credit across the continent - sits at $74 billion in 2025. That number is projected to reach $86 billion by 2027. At the same time, global banks are accelerating their exit from African correspondent banking relationships. Standard Chartered has withdrawn from Angola, Cameroon, Gambia, Sierra Leone, Zimbabwe, and Botswana since 2022. Société Générale exited Morocco and Algeria. BNP Paribas shut its South African investment arm.

The African Continental Free Trade Area is designed to generate $250 billion in additional intra-continental trade. The settlement layer for that commerce does not yet exist.

---

Stablecoins are filling the gap. Not because African businesses became crypto enthusiasts. Because the alternative costs seven percent and takes five days.

Sub-Saharan Africa moved more than $200 billion in on-chain value between mid-2024 and mid-2025. Stablecoins represented 43 percent of that activity. B2B stablecoin payments have grown 60-fold in 30 months. In early 2023, monthly volume was under $100 million. By mid-2025, it exceeded $6 billion. A Lagos-to-Nairobi transfer via wire costs 6 to 8 percent and settles in three to five days. The same transfer via stablecoin costs 1.5 to 2.5 percent and settles in 60 seconds.

Yellow Card, a Nigerian-born infrastructure company operating across 34 countries, exited retail entirely in 2025 to focus on B2B clients. Enterprise demand for cross-border payment infrastructure was growing faster than it could serve. Visa has processed over $225 million in stablecoin settlements through Yellow Card's rails. Mastercard followed with a partnership covering B2B payments, treasury management, and cross-border transfers. These are not crypto partnerships. They are settlement infrastructure moves by the two largest payment networks on earth.

Kotani Pay built a different part of the stack. Its blockchain-to-mobile-money API runs on USSD. USSD works on feature phones with no internet connection. It routes stablecoin-backed payouts to recipients without smartphones or bank accounts. The on-ramp - converting local currency into stablecoins and back - remains the hardest part of the infrastructure to solve. Kotani Pay is solving it specifically for East and West Africa.

---

The infrastructure argument is different from the adoption argument.

Adoption asks whether African businesses will choose crypto. Infrastructure asks which settlement layer moves value cheaply enough that businesses use it without thinking about the rails. The second question has a different answer.

Businesses do not need to believe in stablecoins. They need to pay suppliers in Nairobi from Lagos without losing a week and seven percent. If stablecoins solve that problem more reliably than a wire transfer, the adoption question becomes irrelevant.

The builders who understand this will not build wallets or exchanges. They will build settlement rails, on-ramp APIs, and treasury management layers. The goal is to make stablecoin infrastructure feel like a bank transfer - at a fraction of the cost. Africa's cross-border payments market is projected to reach $1 trillion by 2035, up from $329 billion today. The correspondent banking system meant to serve it is contracting. The gap is where the next generation of African fintech is building.

The Lagos textile exporter does not need a crypto thesis. She needs her payment in Nairobi on Tuesday.
