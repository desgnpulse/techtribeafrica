A Lagos textile exporter ships goods to Nairobi. The payment takes five days and costs seven percent.

That is not a technology problem. It is a settlement infrastructure problem. And it has a $74 billion price tag.

Africa's intra-continental trade sits at 16 percent of the continent's total commerce. The European Union moves 70 percent internally. The gap is not ambition or product-market fit. It is the settlement layer. It was designed for trade with Europe, not trade within Africa.

Global banks are making this worse. Standard Chartered, Société Générale, and BNP Paribas have all exited African correspondent banking operations since 2022. The African Continental Free Trade Area is designed to grow intra-continental commerce. The infrastructure to settle that commerce is contracting.

Stablecoins are filling the gap. Not because African founders believe in crypto. Because a Lagos-to-Nairobi stablecoin transfer costs 1.5 to 2.5 percent and settles in 60 seconds. Yellow Card exited retail in 2025 to go all-in on B2B cross-border settlement. Visa has processed $225 million through its rails. Mastercard followed.

The founders who understand this are not building wallets. They are building settlement rails.

The Lagos exporter does not need a crypto thesis. She needs her payment on Tuesday.
