Firms in Sub-Saharan Africa with unreliable power lose up to 31 percent of sales to outages. The least-affected average 10 hours without power per month. The most affected average 200 hours.

The developers who built software for these markets faced a choice: design for connectivity as the default and fail when the power drops, or design for power interruption as the default and keep working.

The ones who chose the second option built something interesting.

Offline-first architecture puts data and logic on the device. The app works without a connection. It syncs when connectivity returns - whether the drop was from power failure, poor signal, or lost coverage. That is not a workaround. It is a design decision that makes software more reliable in unstable environments than always-on software is in stable ones.

South Africa had 308 consecutive days without load shedding in March 2026. The same period produced 91,934 unique grid outages. Generation improved. Distribution did not. The constraint did not disappear. It shifted.

Offline sync. Local-first data. Graceful degradation. These patterns are not niche African concerns. They are the design requirements of software that must work at the edge of infrastructure.

The African developer who built for 200 monthly hours of outage does not need to re-architect for edge compute. The constraint already produced the solution.
