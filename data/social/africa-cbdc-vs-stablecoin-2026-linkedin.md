Nigeria spent four years building the eNaira. As of late 2024, 98.5 percent of all registered wallets had never been used.

In the same country, 11.9 percent of the population actively uses USDT. That is 25.9 million people.

This is not a story about product-market fit. It is a story about institutional trust.

The naira lost approximately 70 percent of its value between 2021 and 2024. Nigerian adults who lived through that devaluation are being asked to hold a digital version of the same naira, issued by the same central bank. The digital form does not dissolve that history. It carries it.

Stablecoins solve a different problem. USDT and USDC are dollar-denominated, issued by private entities, and already built into the platforms people use daily. A stablecoin does not ask citizens to trust their government. It asks them to open an app.

The signal for African builders is in the architecture of what won. CBDCs failed because they separated the technology from the trust problem. The opportunity now is to build the private-sector infrastructure layer that CBDCs were supposed to deliver. Compliance-ready corridors. On-ramp tooling on feature phones. Treasury tools for businesses that need USD stability without dollar accounts.

The CBN's 2025 pivot to cNGN, a private-sector naira stablecoin, is the clearest admission yet that the market has settled this question.

What does this mean for the fintech builders in your network who are still deciding which rails to build on?
