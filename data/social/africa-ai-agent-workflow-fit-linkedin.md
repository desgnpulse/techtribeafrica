Safaricom Zuri handles thousands of customer queries per day. MTN Nigeria's Zigi does the same across WhatsApp, Telegram, and Facebook Messenger. Both are live systems at telecom scale. Neither is a pilot.

Eighty-eight percent of African organisations are now embedding AI agents. Few ask whether the workflow fits before they deploy.

The cases that are working share three properties: high-frequency volume on a channel the business already owns, repetitive resolution patterns, and transaction economics where human handling costs more than the transaction justifies.

Pumpkn, a Nigerian fintech, automated 70 percent of manual workflow with a WhatsApp-native agent. Completion rates hit 74 percent. Monthly inbound leads exceeded 150. Drop-off re-engagement reached 65 percent. That did not happen because they chose the right model. It happened because the workflow passed all three tests.

The cases that do not work share a different problem. At the 2025 Digital Procurement Africa Summit, a Lagos enterprise director said it plainly: AI cannot have impact where the underlying data does not exist.

The fit test is not complicated. Which workflows in the product already generate high-frequency, repetitive resolution patterns that currently cost human hours? That list is short. The agent candidates are on it. Everything else should wait.
